Bankruptcy Exemptions
Colorado

an informational resource provided by Clean Slate Colorado Â· 970-818-3052
Information current as of April 2018

Bankruptcy can be scary. You're already not answering the phone because you know it's just another creditor asking for money you don't have. Now you've decided to take care this problem by filing for bankruptcy protection, which could be a fantastic turning point in your life, but now you're worried that some of the things you need to survive might get taken in the bankruptcy. Fortunately, the bankruptcy code provides a certain amount of relief from this particular worry. It's called Exemptions.

Yes, once you file a Chapter 7 bankruptcy petition, everything you own is subject to being taken and sold to pay off some of your debt. However, you get to protect a whole bunch of things using Exemptions. Exemptions essentially take the covered property back out of the bankruptcy estate (If you don't know about the bankruptcy estate, you should ask your attorney. It's important to understand.)

This site provides a description of the exemptions provided for debtors in the State of Colorado. Folks who live in Colorado and file for bankruptcy protection may use these rules to protect their assets from being seized by the trustee. Every state has its own rules, so don't assume any of these particular rules apply anywhere but Colorado. There are also complicated timing rules to figure out if you are allowed to use Colorado's exemptions even if you've lived here for a couple years. You may have to use another state's exemptions or even the federal exemptions depending on your situation.

This site was created by an attorney licensed to practice in Colorado state and federal courts. The information here is not legal advice though. These things can get real complicated. If you have any questions, you should ask an attorney to help you figure out how these rules work.

One interesting thing: these rules can also be used in non-bankruptcy debt collection scenarios as well. In fact, these exemptions do not mention bankruptcy in the state code. The bankruptcy code just uses a neat constitutional waiver of federal law preemption here to permit states to make their own rules. (sorry, nerd lawyer moment there)

Homestead

Homestead - Standard

$75,000

Colorado provides a standard homestead exemption of $75,000 provided the property is actually occupied by the debtor/owner or her family. This amount may not be doubled for spouses.

C.R.S. § § 38-41-201, 38-41-202 In re Howe, Bankr.Colo. No. 81 B 03865 C

Homestead - Mobile Homes

$75,000, $105,000, or unlimited

Colorado recognizes mobile homes, manufactured homes, trailers, and trailer coaches as "homes" for the purpose of the homestead exemption with all the rights and limitations of a traditional home. Mobile homes and manufactured homes must have been purchased after January 1, 1983 to qualify; trailers and trailer coaches must have been purchased after July 1, 2000.

C.R.S. § 38-41-201.6

Homestead - Disabled or Elderly

$105,000

Colorado extends the homestead exemption to $105,000 for the following debtors:

  • elderly owners
  • disabled owners
  • an owner who lives with an elderly spouse
  • an owner who lives with a disabled spouse
  • an owner who lives with an elderly dependent
  • an owner who lives with a disabled dependent

for this exemption, "elderly" means 60 years or older

for this exemption, "disabled" is a multi-factor test, which means you'll have to prove it and perhaps make an argument.

C.R.S. § 38-41-201(1)(b)

Homestead - Surviving Spouse or Children

Same as the deceased owner

Colorado permits the surviving spouse or minor children to assert a deceased owner's homestead exemption.

C.R.S § 38-41-204

Burial Plot

Unlimited

Colorado provides full exemption for burial plots for the debtor and each dependent.

C.R.S § 13-54-102(1)(d)

wages

Wages

75% - 25% subject to levy

Colorado exempts 75% of wages. This means that the bankruptcy trustee can take up to 25% of pre-petition wages, even if you were paid the wages after you file. This makes the date of your filing very important to figuring out how this exemption applies.

If your exempt wages are less than 30 hours of the current minimum wage, you can exempt up to that amount regardless of the 25% rule. So for an easy example, if you make $10.20 per hour (current Colorado minimum wage) and work 30 hours a week, your entire paycheck could be exempt. This gets complicated based on filing dates, lookback periods, and other things, so make sure to get an attorney's help with this.

Oh yeah, wages means money you earn for working. If your income comes from other sources, there may be other (and better) exemptions that apply. Keep reading …

C.R.S § 13-54-104

Insurance

Ok, insurance is complicated all by itself. Try to figure out how it works in bankruptcy? Oh boy! Here are the rules in Colorado. If you don't get it, it may not apply to you. Definitely ask for help if you think it might.

Life Insurance - Group Policies

100%

Colorado exempts 100% of the debtor's ownership interest in a group lief insurance policy. These are the types of policies folks get as a benefit from their employer. They usually aren't worth anything unless the person dies, so this exemption is rarely used. It does cover the proceeds of the policy if it does pay a death benefit though, so beneficiaries don't have to use the insurance money to pay off any debt.

C.R.S § 10-7-205

Life Insurance - Contractual Restrictions

100%

Colorado allows a full exemption for an insurance contract, including those for annuities, to restrict the rights of anyone other than the policy owner to take the value or benefits of the policy. This is highly specific to the language in the contract, and may or may not apply depending on the how the specific policy contract is written.

C.R.S § 10-7-106

Life Insurance - Cash Surrender Value

$250,000 if owned for 4 years

Colorado allows an exemption of $250,000 for the cash surrender value of a life insurance policy, but only if the policy has been owned for more than 4 years. There's an exemption for the amount of the cash value that is a result of contributions to the insurance policy over and above the premium that have been made in the the past 4 years.

This one doesn't make sense to a lot of people, and that's okay. Some people use life insurance as an investment rather than as a way to provide death benefits. If you aren't investing in life insurance, this doesn't apply to you. If you own a whole life, universal life, or other similar policy, figuring this exemption will take some work.

C.R.S § 13-54-102(1)(l)(I)(A)

Insurance Proceeds from Loss

100% … sort of

Colorado allows an exemption of the value of insurance payments for loss, destruction or damage to personal property that is also exempt.

This exemption is limited to the exemption that the property would have had if it hadn't been lost.

C.R.S § 13-54-102(1)(m)

Insurance Proceeds from Loss of Homestead

100% … sort of

Colorado allows an exemption of the value of insurance payments for loss, destruction or damage to real property that is also exempt.

This exemption is limited to the exemption that the property would have had if it were sold.

C.R.S § 38-41-209

Accident or Sickness (Disability) Insurance Proceeds

$200 per month or 100%

Colorado allows an exemption of $200 of monthly payments for disability insurance benefits.

If you sue to recover for the accident and get a settlement or win, then the proceeds of the suit are 100% exempt.

C.R.S § 10-16-212

Automobile

Motor Vehicles

$7,500

Colorado allows an exemption of $7,500 to apply towards up to two motor vehicles.

Joint debtors are allowed to double this exemption.

This exemption does not apply to snowmobiles, ATVs, golf carts, boats or other watercraft, travel trailers, tent trailers, or motor homes. Although the homestead exemption might apply if you're living in the trailer or motor home.

C.R.S § 13-54-102(1)(j)(I)

Motor Vehicles - Elderly or Disabled

$12,500

Colorado allows an exemption of $12,500 to apply towards up to two motor vehicles if the debtor is elderly or disabled. This exemption also applies if the debtor has an elderly or disabled spouse or dependent.

Joint debtors are allowed to double this exemption.

This exemption does not apply to snowmobiles, ATVs, golf carts, boats or other watercraft, travel trailers, tent trailers, or motor homes. Although the homestead exemption might apply if you're living in the trailer or motor home.

C.R.S § 13-54-102(1)(j)(II)

Retirement

This type of asset is covered under both state and federal exemptions. Unlike most other things, you can probably use the federal exemptions on these assets regardless of the rules in your state. These are the state rules. The federal rules on retirement accounts are listed later.

ERISA Retirement Accounts (Employee Programs)

$100

Colorado allows a full exemption for retirement accounts that qualify under ERISA (29 U.S.C. § 1002)

C.R.S § 13-54-102(1)(s)

Individual Retirement Accounts (IRAs)

100%

Colorado allows a full exemption for retirement accounts that qualify as traditional IRAs (26 U.S.C § 408), Roth IRAs (26 U.S.C. § 408A).

C.R.S § 13-54-102(1)(s)

Other Defined Benefit Retirement Accounts, i.e. 401(k)

100%

Colorado allows a full exemption for retirement accounts that qualify under 26 U.S.C § 401. This covers the typical defined contribution 401(k) that most folks are familiar with.

C.R.S § 13-54-102(1)(s)

Public Employees

100%

Colorado allows a full exemption for retirement accounts in the Colorado Public Employee Retirement Association (PERA). The exemption applies regardless of whether the account is a defined benefit or defined contribution plan.

There a few exeptions to this exemption for things like fraud, restitution, and child support.

C.R.S § 24-51-212

Police Pensions

100%

Colorado provides a state-funded pension plan specifically for firemen and police officers. A debtor's interest in this pension plan and payments from it are fully exempt.

There a few exeptions to this exemption for things like fraud, restitution, and child support.

C.R.S § 31-30.5-208 C.R.S § 31-31-203

Firefighter Pensions

100%

Colorado provides a state-funded pension plan specifically for firemen and police officers. A debtor's interest in this pension plan and payments from it are fully exempt.

There a few exeptions to this exemption for things like fraud, restitution, and child support.

C.R.S § 31-30.5-208 C.R.S § 31-31-203

Public Benefits

Earned Income Tax Credit (EIC or EITC) and Child Credit

100%

Colorado provides a full exemption for the federal Earned Income Tax Credit and the federal Child Tax Credit.

There a few exeptions to this exemption for things like fraud, restitution, and child support.

C.R.S § 13-54-102(o)

Disability Benefits

$4,000 per month

Colorado provides a full exemption for disability benefits (not insurance) up to a limit of $4,000 per month. Disability payments above $4,000 are handled just like wages.

There a few exeptions to this exemption for things like fraud, restitution, and child support.

C.R.S § 13-54-102(o)

Workers' Compensation

100%

Ok, just like the rest of the workers' compensation program, this one is complicated. If you're getting or trying to get workers' comp., then you should definitely talk with an attorney to see how this one works. Most of these exemptions have a paragraph or two in the Colorado code. This one has 8 long paragraphs.

The way to summarize this: you get full exemption, if you and your employer qualify.

C.R.S § 8-42-124

Public Assistance

100%

Colorado provides a public assistance program for folks who's income falls under certain levels or have a qualifying disability. Payments of benefits under this program are fully exempt.

C.R.S § 26-2-131

Unemployment Benefits

100%

Colorado provides a full exemption for benefits received under the state's unemployment program

The child support exception applies to this exemption.

C.R.S § 8-80-103

Benefits from Crime Victims' Compensation Fund

100%

Colorado provides a program to reimburse victims of certain crimes for their loss. The benefits paid out of this program are fully exempt.

C.R.S § § 24-4.1-114, 13-54-102(1)(q) C.R.S § 13-54-102(1)(q)

Personal Injury Settlement or Reimbursement

100% … sort of

Colorado provides a full exemption for personal injury settlements and damages awards.

This exemption is reduced by any amounts that the debtor owes for treatment for the injury or for collection of the reimbursement (i.e. attorney's fees). Because this exception to the exemption (see what I did there) can be tricky to calculate, you should get help from an attorney if this one applies to you.

C.R.S § 13-54-102(1)(n)

Fraternal Society Benefits

100%

Colorado provides a full exemption for benefits a debtor receives as a member of a fraternal society. The I.R.S has specific rules for how an organization must organize itself in order to qualify as a fraternal society. This exemption is rarely used these days, but it remains on the books.

C.R.S § 10-14-403
