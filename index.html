Bankruptcy Exemptions
Colorado

an informational resource provided by Clean Slate Colorado Â· 970-818-3052

Bankruptcy can be scary. You're already not answering the phone because you know it's just another creditor asking for money you don't have. Now you've decided to take care this problem by filing for bankruptcy protection, which could be a fantastic turning point in your life, but now you're worried that some of the things you need to survive might get siezed in the bankruptcy. Fortunately, the bankruptcy code provides a certain amount of relief from this particular worry. It's called Exemptions.

Yes, once you file a Chapter 7 bankruptcy petition, everything you own is subject to being taken and sold to pay off some of your debt. However, you get to protect a whole bunch of things using Exemptions. Exemptions essentially take the covered property back out of the bankruptcy estate (If you don't know about the bankruptcy estate, you should ask your attorney. It's important to understand)

This site provides a description of the exemptions provided for debtors in the State of Colorado. Folks who live in Colorado and file for bankruptcy protection may use these rules to protect their assets from being seized by the trustee. Every state has its own rules, so don't assume any of these particular rules apply anywhere but Colorado.

This site was created by an attorney licensed to practice in Colorado state and federal courts. The information here is not legal advice though. These things can get real complicated. If you have any questions, you should ask an attorney to help you figure out how these rules work.

One interesting thing: these rules can also be used in non-bankruptcy debt collection scenarios as well. In fact, these exemptions do not mention bankruptcy in the state code. The bankruptcy code just uses a neat constitutional waiver of federal law preemption here to permit states to make their own rules. (sorry, nerd lawyer moment there)

Homestead

Homestead - Standard

$75,000

Colorado provides a standard homestead exemption of $75,000 provided the property is actually occupied by the debtor/owner or her family. This amount may not be doubled for spouses.

C.R.S. § § 38-41-201, 38-41-202

Homestead - Mobile Homes

$75,000, $105,000, or unlimited

Colorado recognizes mobile homes, manufactured homes, trailers, and trailer coaches as "homes" for the purpose of the homestead exemption with all the rights and limitations of a traditional home. Mobile homes and manufactured homes must have been purchased after January 1, 1983 to qualify; trailers and trailer coaches must have been purchased after July 1, 2000.

C.R.S. § 38-41-201.6

Homestead - Disabled or Elderly

$105,000

Colorado extends the homestead exemption to $105,000 for the following debtors:

  • elderly owners
  • disabled owners
  • an owner who lives with an elderly spouse
  • an owner who lives with a disabled spouse
  • an owner who lives with an elderly dependent
  • an owner who lives with a disabled dependent

for this exemption, "elderly" means 60 years or older

for this exemption, "disabled" is a multi-factor test, which means you'll have to prove it and perhaps make an argument.

C.R.S. § 38-41-201(1)(b)

Homestead - Surviving Spouse or Children

Same as the deceased owner

Colorado permits the surviving spouse or minor children to assert a deceased owner's homestead exemption.

C.R.S § 38-41-204

Burial Plot

Unlimited

Colorado provides full exemption for burial plots for the debtor and each dependent

C.R.S § 13-54-102(d)
